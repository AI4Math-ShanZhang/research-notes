How co-visibility is computed — the exact steps

This is precisely what the slider demo runs each frame. For one pair of patches u (camera A) and u′ (camera B):

  1. Build each cone. Apex = the camera's optical center p₀. Axis v̂ = the unit direction from that center through the patch's pixel, computed from the camera's intrinsics (focal length, principal point) and extrinsics (world position + orientation). Half-angle γ = the angular size of one patch (optionally widened by a factor l ≥ 1).
  2. March along each axis inside the depth band. A point on cone A is pₐ(t) = p₀ᴬ + t·v̂ᴬ for depth t ∈ [d_min, d_max]; same for cone B with depth s. The band handles the fact that you don't know how far the object is.
  3. Find the closest approach. Solve for the depths (t*, s*) — one on each axis — that make the two axes come nearest, then clamp t*, s* into [d_min, d_max].
  4. Compute the cone radius there. A cone widens with depth: radius r = t·tan(γ). So r₁ = t*·tan(γ) and r₂ = s*·tan(γ).
  5. Overlap test. The cones intersect (patches are co-visible) if the gap between the two closest points is no bigger than the two radii combined:
    ‖pₐ(t*) − p_b(s*)‖ ≤ r₁ + r₂.
  6. Fill the mask. M_sc[u,u′] = 1 if co-visible (attention allowed), else 0 (attention gets a −∞ bias → blocked). Same-camera pairs are always 1 (the diagonal). Done for all pairs in parallel, once per sample.

The two knobs you actually tune: the cone width γ·l and the depth band [d_min, d_max]. Both control leniency — bigger cones / wider band → more pairs pass as co-visible. Everything else is fixed by calibration. And it's all training-only: at inference the mask is dropped so the robot needs no live calibration.

Reading Figure 4 — what are x and y?

Figure 4 has two panels. (a) is the sight-cone mask you just played with — a big allowed/blocked grid over patch pairs. (b) is the tube mask, and that's where x and y show up. Here's the key:

x = the video latents being generated (the "states")
The stack labelled x_t, x_{t+1}, x_{t+2}… is the sequence of video frames the model is denoising — the compact codes it is trying to produce. These are the "states"/tokens flowing through the network. (During training they're the noised targets; the model's job is to clean them up.)
y = the conditioning channel (the hint / observed content)
y is the conditioning fed alongside x — the known/observed information (inpainting-style, "InP") that guides what to generate. Think of it as "here's what you already know about the scene." It rides along the frames but is given, not generated.

What the "tube" does with x and y

x — latents. A tube = the same small k×k window, held across all frames on one chosen view, is replaced with pure noise.
y — conditioning. With a nested probability, the same tube is also masked here, so the model can't peek at the answer in the hint either.
Why do this? Normally the model could recover a masked patch by the easy route — copying from the same view's other frames (a temporal shortcut) or from the conditioning y. By blanking the whole tube through time in x (and sometimes in y too), both shortcuts are cut. The only way left to reconstruct that region is to attend to the other cameras — which forces cross-view attention to actually be learned and used. The reconstruction loss on that tube is even up-weighted to push harder.

So the two masks are a team: sight-cone says which cross-view pairs are geometrically allowed; tube creates a need to use them. Both are training-only.
