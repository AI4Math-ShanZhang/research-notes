LingBot-VA — §3.3 Training & §3.4 Deployment

Two ideas, kept simple: teacher forcing (how it's trained) and the deployment trick (how it runs fast on a real robot).

Quick reminder: LingBot-VA weaves video and action into one sequence and predicts the next pieces from the past. §3.3 is how you train that; §3.4 is how you run it fast enough for a real robot. Both boil down to a couple of clever tricks.

§3.3 — Teacher Forcing

The idea, in plain words

To train a model that generates a sequence (frame, action, frame, action…), you teach it to predict each next piece from the pieces before it. But what do you feed as "the pieces before it"?

  • Option A — use the model's own guesses. Risky: if it guessed step 3 wrong, step 4 is built on a mistake, and errors snowball.
  • Option B — use the real answers from the dataset. Clean: every step is predicted from correct context, so the model learns each step properly.

Teacher forcing = Option B. During training you always feed the ground-truth previous frames/actions as context, never the model's own predictions.

Everyday analogy Like learning to cook from a recipe where, after every step, you're handed the correctly prepared result of the previous step — instead of building on your own possibly-burnt attempt. You learn each step cleanly.

The usual catch — and why robots dodge it

Teacher forcing normally has a downside called train–test mismatch: at test time there are no ground-truth answers, so the model must feed itself its own predictions — a situation it never practised. Its small errors then compound (this is why text generators can drift).

The key insight for robots: a robot gets real observations from the world after every action. So at deployment the "ground truth" (the real camera view) is available — exactly like training. The robot naturally retrieves real feedback, so teacher forcing matches deployment and the mismatch mostly disappears.
Why it fits a robot A text model has to imagine the whole story alone. A robot doesn't — it looks after each move and sees what really happened. That real glimpse plays the role of the "ground-truth previous step," so training and deployment line up.

Bonus: it trains fast (the causal mask)

Because the whole sequence's ground truth is known during training, the model can predict all positions at once in a single pass, using a causal attention mask — a rule that lets each token look only at earlier tokens, never future ones (Figure 3). That "look only backward" rule keeps it honest (the present depends only on the past) and lets training run in parallel.

Causal mask analogy Like a rule in an exam: you may use your notes from earlier questions, but you can't peek at the answers to later ones. Everyone answers all questions at once, but nobody cheats forward in time.

Two smaller §3.3 tricks (quick)

Noisy History Augmentation. During training they add noise to the past video frames. Why? So the action decoder learns to read actions from partially noisy video. Then at test time you only need to clean the video halfway (not fully) before decoding actions — halving the video denoising steps for a big speedup, with no loss in action quality.

Two training losses. Just like WALL-WM, it's flow matching, not next-token: a dynamics loss (predict the future video) + an inverse-dynamics loss (decode the action from the video), combined as L = L_dyn + λ·L_inv.

§3.4 — The Deployment Trick (run it fast)

Predicting video is slow (many denoising steps). On a real robot that needs ~real-time control, waiting for each prediction is a problem. §3.4 is a chain of three fixes, ending in the clever one.

Fix 0 — KV cache (don't recompute the past)

Since each step conditions on the whole history, they cache the computation of past tokens and reuse it — only the newest tokens need fresh work. Standard autoregressive speedup.

The problem — Synchronous (robot waits)

Naively: the robot executes an action, then stops and waits while the model computes the next one, then executes, then waits… The robot sits idle. Jerky and slow.

Robot
execute a₁idle 💤execute a₂idle 💤
Model
compute a₂compute a₃
Robot idles half the time → too slow

Fix 1 — Asynchronous (overlap execute & predict)

The idea: while the robot executes the current chunk, the model predicts the next chunk in parallel. The robot never stops — prediction latency is hidden behind execution.

Robot
execute a₁execute a₂execute a₃
Model
predict a₂predict a₃predict a₄

Now the two rows run at the same time — no idle. But there's a subtle bug…

The naive-async bug (stale hallucination): to predict the next chunk while executing, the model can't wait for the real new camera view (it hasn't arrived yet), so it uses its own predicted video for "now." The video model loves smoothness, so it just keeps continuing its own imagined video and ignores the real world → the robot slowly drifts off (open-loop degradation).
Model uses
its own STALE predicted frame 🚫 (ignores reality)
Fast, but drifts — stops reacting to what's really there

Fix 2 — FDM-grounded Async (the actual trick) ✅

The fix: before predicting ahead, do a quick Forward Dynamics Model (FDM) step to re-anchor on reality. Instead of trusting the stale forecast, the model takes the most recent real observation plus the action it's currently doing, and "imagines" the resulting frame — a short physics forward pass grounded in the latest real glimpse. That feedback-grounded frame (not the stale one) goes into the cache, so every prediction re-aligns with the real world first.

Model uses
latest REAL obs+current action→FDM: updated frame ✅
Fast AND stays grounded in reality → robust closed-loop control
Driving analogy (the whole §3.4 in one picture) Synchronous = stop the car at every turn to think — safe but painfully slow.
Naive async = keep driving while planning the next 3 turns, but plan from where you imagined you'd be, eyes closed → you drift into the wrong lane.
FDM-grounded async = keep driving and glance at the road first (real observation), update where you actually are, then plan ahead. Fast and on-course.
