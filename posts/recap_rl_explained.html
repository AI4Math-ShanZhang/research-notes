RL for VLAs, unpacked: the RECAP method (π0.6)

A beginner's walkthrough of the Preliminaries & Method — every formula paired with a plain reading and a concrete case.
Physical Intelligence · arXiv 2511.14759 RL · advantage conditioning flow-matching VLA

The paper's core idea is one sentence: train a critic with reinforcement-learning ideas, but train the policy with plain supervised imitation — feeding the critic's judgment in as an extra label. This page builds up to that, starting from zero RL knowledge.

0The three characters

Every RL setup for a robot has three roles. Hold these in your head; all the math just formalizes their conversation.

The Policy
\(\pi(a\mid o)\)
The VLA itself. "Given what I see (\(o\)), what action (\(a\)) do I take?" This is the thing we ultimately deploy on the robot.
The Value function
\(V(o)\)
A critic. "From this situation, how well is it going to go?" It never controls the robot — it only judges.
The Reward
\(r\)
The score. In π0.6: did the task succeed / how fast is it progressing? Sparse and outcome-based.
Why the split matters The policy is a giant flow-matching VLA — hard and unstable to optimize with reward directly. The value function is smaller and trained with a clean classification loss. RECAP puts all the "RL" into the value function, then lets the policy learn by imitation. That is the whole game.

1Preliminaries — the standard RL vocabulary

1.1   Policy and trajectory

\[ \pi(a_t \mid o_t) \qquad\qquad \tau = (o_0, a_0, o_1, a_1, \dots, o_T) \]
Read: the policy picks action \(a_t\) from observation \(o_t\). A trajectory \(\tau\) is one full rollout — "one complete attempt at the task."
\[ \rho_\pi(\tau) = p(o_0)\;\prod_{t=0}^{T-1} \underbrace{\pi(a_t\mid o_t)}_{\text{you act}}\;\underbrace{p(o_{t+1}\mid o_t,a_t)}_{\text{world reacts}} \]
Read: the probability of a whole attempt = start somewhere × repeatedly (your policy acts × physics responds). Two sources of randomness: your policy and the world.

1.2   Reward and return

\[ r_t = r(o_t, a_t) \qquad\qquad R(\tau) = \sum_{t=0}^{T} r_t \]
Read: \(r_t\) is the score at each step (\(r_T\) is the final outcome). The return \(R(\tau)\) is the total score over the whole attempt.
Concrete case — folding laundry An episode of "fold the t-shirt." Return \(R(\tau)\) is high if the shirt ends up neatly folded, low if it stays crumpled. (π0.6 actually uses a shaped signal — steps-to-success — so progress is rewarded, not just the final frame. More in §3.)

1.3   The goal of RL

\[ J(\pi) = \mathbb{E}_{\tau\sim\rho_\pi}\big[\,R(\tau)\,\big] \]
Read: find a policy that, on average across attempts, gets a high return. That average (expectation \(\mathbb E\)) is the entire objective of RL.

1.4   The Value function — "what is this moment worth?"

\[ V^\pi(o_t) = \mathbb{E}\Big[\, \sum_{t'=t}^{T} r_{t'} \,\Big] \]
Read: the expected return-to-go from state \(o_t\), if you keep acting with policy \(\pi\). This is "the value" you asked about — a single number saying "how good is this exact situation."
Concrete case — the value curve (paper Fig. 4) Play back a successful fold: the value rises steadily toward 0 (their convention: 0 = done, negative = steps still remaining). Play back a failure where the robot tips over a water filter: the value drops sharply at that instant. The critic reads the episode like a progress bar.

1.5   The Advantage — "was this action better than average?"

\[ A^\pi(o_t,a_t) = \underbrace{\mathbb{E}\Big[\textstyle\sum_{t'=t}^{t+N-1} r_{t'} + V^\pi(o_{t+N})\Big]}_{\text{what actually happened + value after}} \;-\; \underbrace{V^\pi(o_t)}_{\text{what you expected}} \]
Read: take the reward you actually collected over the next \(N\) steps, plus the value of where you ended up, then subtract the value you expected before acting. This is an "n-step" estimate.
Sign of \(A\)MeaningWhat we do with it
A > 0this action did better than the policy's averagereinforce it — mark it "good"
A < 0this action did worse than average — a mistakesuppress it — mark it "bad"
Concrete case — the fridge / water filter Robot reaches for the fridge and tips over the water filter. The real future got worse than expected → advantage goes negative exactly at that move. It recovers and completes the task → advantage climbs back positive. Advantage is a per-action verdict: did this specific move help or hurt?

2Regularized RL & "the reference" \(\pi_{\text{ref}}\)

Pure reward-chasing is dangerous when you train many gradient steps on a fixed, pre-collected dataset (the "offline" setting). The policy drifts toward strange actions the data never shows, where the critic's estimates are unreliable. The fix: keep the new policy close to a reference policy \(\pi_{\text{ref}}\) — usually the behavior that collected the data.

\[ J(\pi,\pi_{\text{ref}}) = \mathbb{E}\Big[\textstyle\sum_t \gamma^t r_t\Big] \;-\; \beta\,\mathbb{E}_{o}\big[\,D\big(\pi(\cdot\mid o)\,\|\,\pi_{\text{ref}}(\cdot\mid o)\big)\big] \]
Read: maximize reward, minus a penalty \(D\) for straying from \(\pi_{\text{ref}}\). \(\beta\) sets the leash length; \(D\) is a divergence (e.g. KL).

2.1   The classic closed-form solution

With KL as the divergence, there is a famous exact answer for the best regularized policy:

\[ \hat\pi(a\mid o) \;\propto\; \pi_{\text{ref}}(a\mid o)\,\exp\!\big(A^{\pi_{\text{ref}}}(o,a)/\beta\big) \]
Read: the optimal policy = the reference, re-weighted by advantage. Actions with high advantage get their probability multiplied up; low-advantage actions get pushed down. This one line is the seed of everything that follows.

2.2   The variant RECAP actually uses

RECAP swaps the exponential for an "improvement probability" \(p(I\mid A)\) — the chance that action \(a\) improves over \(\pi_{\text{ref}}\), via a monotonically increasing function \(g\):

\[ \hat\pi(a\mid o) \;\propto\; \pi_{\text{ref}}(a\mid o)\;\Big(\,p\big(I\mid A^{\pi_{\text{ref}}}(o,a)\big)\Big)^{\beta} \]
Read: same shape as above, but the multiplier is now "how likely is this action to be an improvement?" There's a theorem: this \(\hat\pi\) is guaranteed to be at least as good as \(\pi_{\text{ref}}\) — \(J(\hat\pi)\ge J(\pi_{\text{ref}})\). Guaranteed improvement, no policy-gradient needed.
The pivot to a trainable policy Instead of computing that ratio at test time, they turn it into a learning target: fit a network \(\pi_\theta\) so it matches \(\hat\pi\) — minθ KL(π̂, πθ). Next section shows how this collapses into a shockingly simple supervised loss.

Pre-training runs steps ②③ on tens of thousands of hours of demos. Then ①②③ repeat on the robot's own autonomous attempts (plus expert corrections) to keep improving.

3.2   Step ② — the value function, concretely (Eq. 1)

They don't regress a single number. They predict a distribution over 201 value bins (a distributional critic) and train it with cross-entropy — treating "how good is this?" as a classification problem, which is far more stable.

\[ \min_{\phi}\; \mathbb{E}_{\tau\in\mathcal D}\Big[\,\textstyle\sum_{o_t\in\tau} H\big(R_t^{B}(\tau),\, p_\phi(V\mid o_t,\ell)\big)\Big] \]
Read: for each frame, take the actual return-to-go \(R_t\), bin it (\(R^B_t\), \(B{=}201\) bins), and train the critic \(p_\phi\) to predict that bin via cross-entropy \(H\). \(\ell\) = the language command (multi-task). Recover a scalar value by \(V=\sum_b p_\phi(b)\,v(b)\).
Why "steps-to-success" as the target They normalize the return so 0 = task complete and more-negative = more steps still to go. So the critic literally learns to answer "how many steps until this is done?" — which naturally spikes downward at mistakes (a mistake adds steps). This is a Monte-Carlo estimate: it just uses what actually happened in the logged trajectory, no bootstrapping. Simple and reliable.

3.3   Step ③ — policy extraction via advantage conditioning

Here's the clever collapse. Using Bayes' rule they rewrite the improvement probability as a ratio of two versions of the same reference policy — one that sees an "improvement indicator" \(I\), one that doesn't:

\[ \hat\pi(a\mid o,\ell) \;\propto\; \pi_{\text{ref}}(a\mid o,\ell)\left(\frac{\pi_{\text{ref}}(a\mid I,o,\ell)}{\pi_{\text{ref}}(a\mid o,\ell)}\right)^{\!\beta} \tag{2} \]
Read: the improved policy is built from \(\pi_{\text{ref}}\) with and without a conditioning flag \(I\) ("this action is an improvement"). For the special case \(\beta{=}1\), it simplifies to just \(\hat\pi = \pi_{\text{ref}}(a\mid I,o,\ell)\) — i.e. "the reference policy, told to be good."
This is exactly classifier-free guidance (CFG), reused Just like a diffusion image model is trained both with and without a text prompt, here the policy is trained both with and without the optimality flag \(I\). So you never need to build \(p(I\mid A)\) explicitly — you just train one model that can do both. (Their method is closest to CFGRL.)

The flag \(I\) is a simple binarized advantage — is the advantage above a task-specific threshold \(\epsilon_\ell\)?

\[ I_t \;=\; \mathbb{1}\!\left[\,A^{\pi_{\text{ref}}}(o_t,a_t,\ell) > \epsilon_\ell\,\right] \]
Read: \(I_t = 1\) ("good") if this action's advantage clears the bar, else 0 ("meh"). Turning advantage into a single bit is what makes the whole thing a clean supervised label.

3.4   The final training objective (Eq. 3)

\[ \min_{\theta}\; \mathbb{E}_{\mathcal D_{\pi_{\text{ref}}}}\Big[\, -\log\pi_\theta(a_t\mid o_t,\ell)\;-\;\alpha\,\log\pi_\theta(a_t\mid I_t,o_t,\ell)\,\Big] \]
Read: two imitation terms. Term 1: predict the action without the flag (learn general behavior). Term 2: predict the action with the flag \(I_t\) (learn "what does a GOOD action look like here?"). \(\alpha\) balances them. That's it — it's just weighted behavior cloning with a goodness bit attached.
How the pieces click together at deploy time Training taught the model the mapping flag → behavior. At deployment you hard-set the flag to "good" (\(I=1\)) and the policy emits the high-advantage actions while suppressing the mediocre ones it also saw. The RL lived entirely in the critic and the advantage bit; the policy stayed a stable supervised problem.
Where human corrections plug in When a human teleoperates a fix mid-rollout, RECAP simply forces \(I=\text{True}\) for those actions — "expert corrections are, by assumption, always improvements." No separate machinery: demos, autonomous rollouts, and interventions all flow through the same one-bit objective.

4Traditional PPO vs the proposed method

4.1   How traditional PPO trains a policy

PPO (Proximal Policy Optimization) is the standard on-policy RL workhorse. It directly pushes up the probability of high-advantage actions, with a "trust region" clip so each update stays small:

\[ \mathcal L^{\text{PPO}} = \mathbb{E}\Big[\min\big(\rho_t A_t,\;\text{clip}(\rho_t,1-\epsilon,1+\epsilon)A_t\big)\Big],\qquad \rho_t=\frac{\pi_\theta(a_t\mid o_t)}{\pi_{\text{old}}(a_t\mid o_t)} \]
Read: raise the action's probability in proportion to its advantage \(A_t\), but clip the probability ratio \(\rho_t\) so the policy can't lurch too far in one step. It needs the likelihood ratio \(\rho_t\) to work.
Why PPO is painful for a flow-matching VLA PPO needs a tractable action log-likelihood \(\log\pi_\theta(a\mid o)\) to form \(\rho_t\). But flow-matching / diffusion action experts don't give you one cheaply — they define actions through an iterative denoising process, not a closed-form density. People bolt on approximations (DPPO/FPO), and to keep off-policy training stable they must shrink the trust region so hard (η = 0.01 in the paper) that the policy barely moves.

4.3   What the experiments found

On the T-shirts/Shorts task, both PPO and AWR (a weighted-regression alternative) were given the same data RECAP used. PPO stayed stable only with a tiny trust region and then failed to improve over the offline-RL+SFT starting point. AWR reached a reasonable success rate but produced slow policies (low throughput) because it throws away much of the data. RECAP's keep-all-data, one-bit-label recipe beat both — and on a hard adversarial fold, two RECAP iterations (600 trajectories each) drove success to 97%.
The one-line takeaway PPO tries to make a hard optimization (reward maximization on a density-free model) work. RECAP sidesteps it: push the RL into a stable distributional critic, distill its verdict into a single "is this action good?" bit, and let the VLA learn that bit by ordinary imitation. Same RL benefit, none of the policy-gradient fragility.

5Q&A — the sticking points we worked through

These are the exact questions that came up while unpacking the paper, in the order they arose. Each one was a real point of confusion worth pinning down.

A · Online vs offline, and the value function

Does offline learning skip training the policy?

No — both online and offline train the policy. The split is only about where the data comes from: offline = a fixed saved log; online = fresh data the policy collects itself. RECAP uses both (offline pre-train, then online rollouts + corrections).

Does V judge the observation or the action?

V judges the observation (the situation), not the action. It scores "how well is it going from here." To judge an action you difference two observation-scores → that's the advantage.

Are "critic" and "value function" the same thing?

Yes — one network, two names. "Value function" is the object V; "critic" is its job (judging). There is no separate critic network.

V vs Q?

V(o) scores a situation only; Q(o,a) would score a situation + action. π0.6 deliberately uses V (cheap to fit by replaying logs) and recovers action-quality via the advantage subtraction.

Is a "situation" the same as a trajectory?

No. A situation = one observation ot (a single frame). A trajectory τ = the whole ordered sequence of all frames in one attempt. V eats one situation at a time.

B · The observation and the action (robot I/O)

What's the format of o — just an image?

A bundle: ot = [3 camera images + robot config qt] plus the language command ℓ. Three cameras = one base + one per wrist. Multimodal, not a single image.

Is qt (joint angles + gripper) fixed?

The values change every step (re-sensed from encoders as the arm moves). The structure (how many numbers) is fixed for one robot, but differs across robots — the cross-embodiment challenge.

Are q and a both just ~a handful of numbers?

Yes — both are short float vectors, one number per joint + gripper (π0.6's two-arm robot ≈ 14). But the action output is a chunk = many such vectors stacked (H future steps).

Is q the result of the action?

q is a sensor measurement; a is a motor command. The result of action at is the next config qt+1 — and only approximately, because physics (contact, lag) intervenes. qt itself is an input read to choose at.

So current state + action = next state?

Right idea — that's the dynamics. But π0.6's action is an absolute target, so qt+1 ≈ at (not qt + at). "qt + at" would be a delta action. Either way "=" is really "≈" (physics).

What does "@ 50 Hz" mean?

The arms get a fresh command 50 times per second (every 20 ms) → smooth motion. The model doesn't think that fast: it predicts an action chunk once, then that chunk is streamed to the motors at 50 Hz. "Slow brain, fast body."

C · Computing the advantage (the n-step formula)

What are t+N and T?

t+N = a nearby frame N steps ahead — the handoff point where you stop counting real rewards and let V estimate the rest. T = the actual end of the episode (far away). n-step deliberately stops at t+N, never walks to T.

Does the model infer the N steps, simultaneously or one-by-one?

Neither — it doesn't infer them at all. The N steps already happened and are saved on disk. Computing advantage = reading recorded rewards + running V twice. (Don't confuse with the action chunk, which is live prediction.)

How many times do we call V?

Exactly twice: V(ot+N) and V(ot). The "N" (e.g. 3) refers to reward numbers read from the log, not V calls. Demo: A = [rt+rt+1+rt+2 + V(ot+3)] − V(ot) = (−3 + −1) − (−5) = +1 (good action).

When we call V, do we save the reward?

No. Reward is saved earlier, when the robot ran (from the task outcome — no model involved). V is called later, at training time. Two separate moments; V never produces reward.

D · The reward, and what V learns

Is the reward −1 each step and +1 on the last?

Not +1 — it's −1 per working step, 0 at success. Success = the countdown reaching 0 (you stop paying −1), not a bonus.

Does the reward ignore the final success?

No — success is the anchor. Steps-to-go is measured toward the success frame, so a success makes the countdown reach 0, a failure never reaches it. Steps-to-go IS success, spread across every frame (plus it locates mistakes).

Does V learn action accuracy?

No. V is a progress estimator: situation → predicted steps-to-success. It never sees an action or compares to a "correct" move. Action-accuracy is an imitation idea; RL grades outcomes/progress.

Why train V with Eq. 1?

Eq. 1 builds the progress estimator: it teaches V to match the recorded steps-to-go via cross-entropy. (Eq. 1 trains V; Eq. 3 trains the policy — two networks, two formulas.)

E · The two losses & flow matching

Why Eq. 3 (log-likelihood) and not plain MSE on the action?

Plain MSE averages multiple good actions into a bad one (go-left + go-right → go-straight → crash). A distribution keeps both as separate modes. Eq. 3 also needs a distribution to attach the good/bad flag to.

So is Eq. 3 the MSE / the flow matching?

Three zoom levels of one thing: Eq. 3 = the goal (make the good action likely); flow matching = how the continuous part is trained; velocity-MSE = the arithmetic inside flow matching. The −log π becomes a velocity-MSE when implemented.

Why can't we compute −log π directly for continuous actions?

Flow matching outputs a velocity, and the action is the end of a multi-step denoising journey — there's no direct probability, and recovering one needs an expensive volume-change integral along the whole path. Cheap to sample, not to get a density.

Are the continuous actions the same as action tokens?

Opposite representations. Continuous actions = raw numbers (flow matching, no easy log π). Action tokens = discrete symbols (next-token, easy log π). π0.6 deploys the continuous action; the discrete tokens are a training-only helper ("knowledge insulation").

How does training use both if flow matching only touches continuous actions?

Two losses, added: cross-entropy on the discrete tokens + flow-matching velocity-MSE on the continuous actions. They share a backbone, so the discrete loss keeps the backbone healthy, which helps the action expert.

F · Deployment

How is the action sampled at deployment?

(1) Set the flag to "good" (I=1). (2) Flow-matching sample: start from noise, follow the predicted velocity for a few ODE steps → an action chunk. (3) Execute the chunk at 50 Hz, re-plan before it runs out. Few steps = real-time; no probabilities, just follow the velocity.
