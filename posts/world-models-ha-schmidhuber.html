World Models

Paper: World Models — David Ha & Jürgen Schmidhuber (2018), arXiv:1803.10122. Interactive version: worldmodels.github.io.

📄 Paper Summary

An RL agent is split into a large, unsupervised world model and a tiny controller. The world model learns to compress and predict the environment; the controller just reads from it to pick actions. Three components:

PartNameWhat it does
VVision (VAE)Compresses each raw frame into a small latent vector z.
MMemory (MDN-RNN)A recurrent net that predicts the next latent z from the current z, the action, and its own hidden state h. This is the temporal model of the world.
CControllerA tiny linear model mapping [z, h] → action. Trained by evolution (CMA-ES).
flowchart LR frame[raw frame] --> V[V: VAE] V -->|z| M[M: MDN-RNN memory] V -->|z| C[C: controller] M -->|h| C C -->|action| env[environment] env --> frame

Headline result: because M is a full predictive model of the environment, the agent can train its controller entirely inside M's own imagined rollouts — a "dream" — and then transfer the learned policy back to the real environment. Shown on CarRacing and the VizDoom "Take Cover" task, where the controller was trained purely inside the hallucinated world and still worked in the real game.

🖊 My Key Notes — focus on memory

Why memory (the M model) is the heart of the paper

The authors' framing is cognitive: humans don't act on raw sensory input, we act on an internal predictive model built from experience. That model is spatial and temporal — it remembers how things tend to unfold and predicts what comes next. In the paper, M is exactly that memory:

  • It remembers history in h. The RNN's hidden state h is a compressed summary of everything seen so far. The current frame z alone is not enough (the world is partially observable); h supplies the context — velocity, momentum, what just happened off-screen.
  • It predicts the future, not just recalls the past. M's job is to output the distribution of the next latent z. Memory here means a generative model of dynamics, so the agent can "run the world forward" in its head.
  • It predicts a distribution, not a single frame. M uses a Mixture Density Network (MDN) — it outputs a mixture of Gaussians over the next z. The world is stochastic (a monster may or may not shoot), so a point prediction would be wrong; a distribution captures the uncertainty honestly.
Baseball analogy (theirs). A batter has milliseconds to swing — faster than the visual signal even reaches the conscious brain. They can only hit the ball because an internal model predicts where and when it will arrive. The controller relying on M's h is the same idea: act on the predicted future, not the (already stale) present.

The authors' key insights for world models

  1. Put the intelligence in the model, keep the controller tiny. V and M hold millions of parameters and learn unsupervised (cheap, abundant data); C has only a few hundred. A small controller is why simple evolution strategies can train it — credit assignment is easy when the policy is tiny and the hard representation work is already done by memory.
  2. You can train inside the dream. Once M is a good enough memory of the world, its imagined rollouts are a free, fast simulator. Learn the policy there, transfer to reality. Memory quality directly sets how far you can train without touching the real environment.
  3. A too-perfect dream is dangerous — add uncertainty on purpose. If M is deterministic/overconfident, the controller learns to exploit the model's flaws (adversarial policies that win in the dream but fail for real). The temperature knob τ inflates M's predicted uncertainty, making the dream noisier and harder, which forces a robust policy that survives the reality gap.
  4. Iterative improvement. Because memory and policy co-depend, you can loop: collect data with the current policy → improve M → improve C → repeat, letting the world model grow with the agent's competence.
Takeaway for me: "memory" in a world model isn't a lookup buffer — it's a learned, uncertainty-aware, generative model of how the world evolves. That single idea (M) is what lets the agent imagine, plan, and even train without the real environment. Everything downstream in modern world-model work (Dreamer, etc.) is a descendant of this M-as-predictive-memory framing.
